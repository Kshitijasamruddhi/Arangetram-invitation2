Arangetram

In Tamil language "Arangam" means a stage and "Etram" means to enter. The word "Arangetram" means the debut performance on stage. In South India, it is customary to present the debut recital for a knowledgeable audience when the guru decides that the students have developed the capability to present the art form for an audience. In addition to the Arangetram being an indication for the dance student's achievement of competency in that art form, the guru is responsible for affirming that the dancer has developed the required qualification. In short, it is an assessment of proficiency of both the guru and the student. Margam

Shloka:

In Indian tradition, it is auspicious to seek the blessings of God before starting anything distinctive. This composition praises the inception of the Lord Ganesh, being the destroyer of all obstacles, followed by the Lord Krishna shloka which beautifully captures the essence of Lord Krishna's divine beauty and his adorned state.

Alaripu:

The Alaripu is performed at the start of the margam. The Alarippu means flowering bud. It is a form of pure dance i.e., Nritta. It is an invocatory dance performed at the beginning of the recital, as a way of showing reverence and respect to the God, Guru and the audience and contains various movements of the neck, eyes and head are performed. It introduces the fundamental elements of hasta, pada, shira, drishti and griva in Bharatanatyam.

Ragam: Nattai Talam: Chatushra Ekam


Jatiswaram:

A Jatiswaram is a pure dance presentation, devoid of any abhinaya (emotions), in which intricate sequences are fused with repetitive musical notes. The dance deals with the execution of Adavus and Mudras combined in definite groups. Jatis are executed combining Swara passages in a particular Raga and Taala.

Ragam: Kalyani Talam: Rupak


Shabdam:

This is the first item of Margam where abhinaya are introduced where small jatis are performed intermittently in Shabdam. It is usually in praise of God or King. This dance is a form of storytelling, where performers skillfully convey tales of devotion, love, and mythological stories. The specific composition being showcased focuses on the tales of Krishna such as the downfall of Kansa's pride, Vasudev's Journey to Rescue Balakrishna.

Ragam: Ragmalika Talam: Mishra Chapu


Varnam:

A Varnam is the largest, most important, and challenging central piece of recital in Bharatanatyam. It provides a scope to showcase the expertise in rhythmic dance technique and versatility in Abhinaya of a dancer. 'Hari Ha' is a Nayika Varnam, a composition in the Marathi language, written by 'Sarfoji Raje' of Tanjavur in the 18th century. It portrays the character of Satyabhama, the proud wife of Lord Krishna, in a quarrelsome mood. .

Ragam: AnandBhairavi Talam: Aadi


Vatsalya Padam (Aluvadyatko):

Padam is slow, deep eloquent song expressed only through Abhinay, mostly based on stories of affection and are indicative of the dancer's ability to express a range of human emotions. Main ideology behind Vatsalya Padam is to express love and affection of a mother towards her child. Padam Aluvadyatko Ranga composed by ‘Shri. Purandara Das’ and written in Kannada language. This portrays mother Yashoda trying her best to pacify child Krishna who is crying incessantly by recalling his all Lilas like Putana, Govardhan, Kaliya Mardan etc.

Ragam: Ragmalika Talam: Aadi


Ashtapadi (Madhave):

Ashtapadi means a composition of eight verses which was composed by Jayadeva in the 12th century in Sanskrit hymns of Gita Govinda. These Ashtapadis are integral to the narrative of Radha and Krishna's divine love, beautifully crafted with lyrical elegance and spiritual depth. The story portrays the character of Radha, who is a Kalahantarita nayika filled with arrogant pride, and her sakhi tries to convince her to let go of her anger and meet Krishna.

Ragam: Mohanam Talam: Mishra Chapu


Tillana:

Tillana is a vibrant rhythmic composition symbolizing pure exuberant joy including all types of Adavus, usually containing the second last couplet in praise of the God. The couplet here is in praise of Lord Vishnu the charming and benevolent being.

Ragam: Vridavani Talam: Aadi


Mangalam:

The end of recital, Managalam is concluding and thanksgiving ritual. Reciting the shlokas of all the deities and hoping for the fruitful end. The performers again salute the Almighty, the Guru and the Viewers for making the Performance successful.

Ragam: Madhyamavati Talam: Mishra Chapu

Dhyas Performing Arts

Dhyas performing arts, from past 20 years is successfully progressing in the field of classical and sugam Music and classical dance Bharatanatyam. At dhyas performing arts, we strive to preserve the aesthetics and the values of Indian traditions and culture and inculcate those values in the hearts and souls of the students. Through this endeavor, we intend to inspire and teach technical training of classical music and classical dance Bharatanatyam to aspiring musical and dance talents and give them scope to reach high standards of performance. Besides music and dance exams, the participation of dhyas students in competitions on national and international level is quotable. Along with this, dhyas undertakes several activities and celebrations throughout the year, which help in the overall development of the students.

With the aim to spread and inculcate interest in the field of classical music, Dhyas Performing arts, was established by Mr. Sachin Nevpurkar in the year 2004. From 2006 Mrs. Ketki Nevpurkar Started the Department of Bharatanatyam which further got associated with Kalavardhini Charitable trust, Pune in the year 2011.
